## 1. Background and Introduction

### 1.1 Dysmenorrhea: Definition and Clinical Significance

Dysmenorrhea is defined as painful menstrual cramps of uterine origin and represents one of the most prevalent gynecological disorders among women of reproductive age. It is classified into primary dysmenorrhea (pain in the absence of identifiable pelvic pathology) and secondary dysmenorrhea (pain attributable to underlying pelvic disease such as endometriosis or uterine fibroids) (Ferries-Rowe et al., 2020). Primary dysmenorrhea typically begins within the first few years after menarche and is characterized by cramping lower abdominal pain that radiates to the back and thighs, often accompanied by systemic symptoms including nausea, vomiting, fatigue, and headache (Burnett & Lemyre, 2017). The pain typically begins a few hours before or after the onset of menstruation and lasts for 24–72 hours (Dawood, 2006).

### 1.2 Epidemiology

Dysmenorrhea affects a substantial proportion of menstruating women globally. Prevalence rates range from 45% to 95% depending on the population studied and the diagnostic criteria employed (Ju et al., 2014). It is consistently identified as the leading cause of school and work absenteeism among young women, with significant socioeconomic implications. A systematic review by De Sanctis et al. (2015) reported that approximately 10–20% of adolescents experience severe dysmenorrhea that interferes with daily activities and quality of life. In Nigeria, prevalence rates of 45–75% have been reported among university students (Nwankwo et al., 2021). The global economic burden of dysmenorrhea is substantial, with billions of dollars lost annually in productivity (Barcikowska et al., 2020).

### 1.3 Pathophysiology of Primary Dysmenorrhea

The pathophysiology of primary dysmenorrhea is centered on the overproduction of prostaglandins, particularly prostaglandin F2-alpha (PGF2α) and prostaglandin E2 (PGE2), by the secretory endometrium during menstruation (Dawood, 2006). These prostaglandins are synthesized from arachidonic acid via the cyclooxygenase (COX) pathway. PGF2α is a potent myometrial stimulant and vasoconstrictor; its elevated levels cause:

- **Uterine hypercontractility**: High-amplitude, high-frequency contractions that increase intrauterine pressure to levels exceeding 60 mmHg (Akerlund, 1979)
- **Uterine ischemia**: Vasoconstriction of uterine blood vessels reduces oxygen delivery to the myometrium, leading to hypoxia and pain
- **Sensitization of pain fibers**: Prostaglandins directly sensitize peripheral nociceptors to mechanical and chemical stimuli

The expression of COX-2, the inducible isoform of cyclooxygenase, is markedly upregulated during menstruation, driving the excessive prostaglandin synthesis (Harel, 2006). Additionally, leukotrienes (C4 and D4) and vasopressin have been implicated in augmenting uterine contractility and ischemic pain (Strömberg et al., 1984; Liedman et al., 2006). Elevated levels of PGF2α in the menstrual fluid of women with dysmenorrhea correlate positively with pain severity (Chan et al., 1979).

## 2. Current Pharmacological Management and Limitations

### 2.1 Nonsteroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs)

NSAIDs are considered the first-line pharmacological treatment for primary dysmenorrhea. They exert their therapeutic effect by inhibiting COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes, thereby blocking prostaglandin synthesis (Marjoribanks et al., 2015). A Cochrane systematic review of 80 randomized controlled trials involving 5,820 women demonstrated that NSAIDs were significantly more effective than placebo for pain relief (OR 4.37, 95% CI 3.76–5.09) (Marjoribanks et al., 2015). Commonly used NSAIDs include ibuprofen, naproxen, mefenamic acid, diclofenac, and ketoprofen.

### 2.2 Limitations of NSAID Therapy

Despite their efficacy, NSAIDs are associated with several limitations:

1. **Adverse effects**: The Cochrane review reported that NSAIDs were associated with a significantly higher risk of adverse effects compared to placebo (OR 1.29, 95% CI 1.11–1.51), including gastrointestinal disturbances (OR 1.58, 95% CI 1.12–2.23) and neurological effects such as headache and drowsiness (OR 2.74, 95% CI 1.66–4.53) (Marjoribanks et al., 2015).

2. **Therapeutic failure**: Approximately 18–20% of women with primary dysmenorrhea do not respond adequately to NSAID therapy (Oladosu et al., 2018). This subset of patients is classified as having NSAID-resistant dysmenorrhea.

3. **Timing-dependent efficacy**: NSAIDs must be initiated 1–2 days before the expected onset of menses to achieve complete suppression of prostaglandin synthesis. Delayed administration results in gradual or incomplete suppression (Harel, 2006).

4. **Contraindications**: NSAIDs are contraindicated in women with peptic ulcer disease, renal impairment, bleeding disorders, and aspirin-sensitive asthma. COX-2 selective inhibitors, while associated with fewer gastrointestinal effects, have been linked to cardiovascular adverse events, limiting their use (Marjoribanks et al., 2015).

### 2.3 Hormonal Contraceptives

Combined oral contraceptives and progestin-only preparations represent an alternative first-line option, particularly for women who also require contraception. They reduce menstrual fluid volume and prostaglandin synthesis by suppressing endometrial growth (Wong et al., 2009). However, not all women are candidates for hormonal therapy, and some may experience undesirable side effects including nausea, weight gain, mood changes, and breakthrough bleeding.

### 2.4 The Need for Alternative Therapies

Given the limitations of current pharmacotherapy, including adverse effects, therapeutic failure rates, and contraindications, there is a compelling need for safer, effective alternative treatments for dysmenorrhea. Medicinal plants represent a promising source of such alternatives, and many have a long history of traditional use in managing menstrual disorders (Mirabi et al., 2014; Pattanittum et al., 2016). Systematic reviews have demonstrated that several herbal medicines show efficacy comparable to NSAIDs for dysmenorrhea with fewer side effects (Ramezani & Jalali, 2021; Rahnama & Montazeri, 2021).

## 3. Clove (Syzygium aromaticum): Botanical and Ethnopharmacological Overview

### 3.1 Botanical Classification

Syzygium aromaticum (L.) Merr. & L. M. Perry, commonly known as clove, is an aromatic tree belonging to the family Myrtaceae. It is native to the Maluku Islands (Moluccas) of Indonesia and is now cultivated extensively in tropical regions including Tanzania, Madagascar, Sri Lanka, India, Indonesia, and Zanzibar (Batiha et al., 2020). The dried flower buds constitute the spice known commercially as cloves. The tree can grow to a height of 10–20 meters and begins to produce flower buds after approximately 6–8 years of growth (Mittal et al., 2014).

### 3.2 Traditional and Ethnopharmacological Uses

Clove has been employed in traditional medicine systems across Asia, Africa, and Europe for centuries. Documented traditional uses include:

- **Dental applications**: Topical use for toothache relief and oral infections (Cai & Wu, 1996; Chaieb et al., 2007)
- **Gastrointestinal disorders**: Treatment of dyspepsia, flatulence, nausea, and gastric ulcers (Santin et al., 2011)
- **Analgesic and anti-inflammatory**: Relief of arthritic pain, muscle aches, and inflammatory conditions (Batiha et al., 2020)
- **Gynecological uses**: Traditional use for menstrual cramps, as documented in various ethnobotanical surveys (Ponce-Monter et al., 2017; Tajuddin et al., 2004)
- **Respiratory conditions**: Expectorant and antitussive properties (Xue et al., 2022)
- **Antimicrobial**: Treatment of bacterial, fungal, and parasitic infections (Haro-González et al., 2021)

The traditional use of clove for menstrual pain and uterine cramps provides the ethnopharmacological rationale for investigating its antidysmenorrhea effects (Tajuddin et al., 2004).

## 4. Phytochemistry of Clove

### 4.1 Essential Oil Composition

Clove flower buds contain approximately 15–20% essential oil, with eugenol (4-allyl-2-methoxyphenol) as the predominant bioactive constituent, accounting for 50–90% of the volatile oil (Batiha et al., 2020; Haro-González et al., 2021). The major phytochemical constituents are summarized below:

| Compound | Content (%) | Chemical Class |
|---|---|---|
| Eugenol | 50–90 | Phenylpropanoid |
| Eugenyl acetate | 5–15 | Phenylpropanoid ester |
| β-Caryophyllene | 5–12 | Sesquiterpene |
| α-Humulene | 1–5 | Sesquiterpene |
| Methyl eugenol | Trace | Phenylpropanoid |
| Chavicol | Trace | Phenylpropanoid |

Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) analysis has identified over 30 compounds in clove essential oil, with the composition varying depending on geographical origin, extraction method, and plant part used (Haro-González et al., 2021; Chaieb et al., 2007; Selles et al., 2020).

### 4.2 Non-Volatile Constituents

Beyond the essential oil, clove buds are rich in non-volatile polyphenolic compounds including:

- **Phenolic acids**: Gallic acid, caffeic acid, ferulic acid, ellagic acid, and salicylic acid (Batiha et al., 2020)
- **Flavonoids**: Quercetin, kaempferol, rhamnetin, eugenin, and their glycosides (Ryu et al., 2016; Nassar et al., 2007)
- **Tannins**: Hydrolyzable tannins including eugenin and ellagitannins (Shan et al., 2005)
- **Triterpenoids**: Oleanolic acid, crategolic acid (Mittal et al., 2014)
- **Steroids**: β-Sitosterol, stigmasterol (Xue et al., 2022)

Clove is considered one of the richest dietary sources of polyphenols, with total phenolic content exceeding that of many other commonly consumed spices (Shan et al., 2005). A total of 73 non-volatile components have been reported from S. aromaticum, including 36 flavonoids, 4 chromones, 26 tannins, 3 triterpenoids, 1 coumarin, and 1 phenolic ester (Xue et al., 2022).

### 4.3 Eugenol: The Principal Bioactive Compound

Eugenol (C10H12O2; molecular weight 164.20 g/mol) is a phenylpropanoid characterized by a phenolic ring with a methoxy group at position 2 and a prop-2-enyl side chain at position 4. The United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has classified eugenol as Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) for use as a food additive (Batiha et al., 2020). The Joint WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives (JECFA) has established an acceptable daily intake of 2.5 mg/kg body weight for clove oil (WHO/EMA, 2011). Eugenol is a colorless to pale yellow liquid with a strong aromatic odor and low water solubility (approximately 2,460 mg/L at 25°C) (Haro-González et al., 2021).

## 5. Pharmacological Activities Relevant to Antidysmenorrhea

### 5.1 Anti-Inflammatory Activity

The anti-inflammatory properties of clove and its constituent eugenol have been extensively documented through both in vitro and in vivo studies.

**Prostaglandin synthesis inhibition**: Eugenol has been shown to suppress cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) expression and inhibit prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) production in lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-stimulated RAW264.7 macrophage cells with an IC50 of 0.37 μM (Kim et al., 2003). This study demonstrated that eugenol directly inhibits COX-2 gene expression without affecting COX-1, suggesting a favorable safety profile with respect to gastric mucosal protection. On the contrary, Awouters et al. (1988) demonstrated that eugenol inhibited prostaglandin synthesis in isolated tissues and reduced tone of myometrium and gut muscle.

**Multiple inflammatory mediator suppression**: Eugenol inhibits the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines including tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), interleukin-1 beta (IL-1β), and interleukin-6 (IL-6) in activated macrophages (Dibazar et al., 2015; Rodrigues et al., 2009; Bachiega et al., 2012). It also suppresses nitric oxide (NO) production by downregulating inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) expression (Li et al., 2006). Eugenol modulates the NLRP3 inflammasome pathway and NF-κB expression (Li et al., 2023).

**In vivo anti-inflammatory activity**: The ethanolic extract of S. aromaticum demonstrated significant anti-inflammatory activity in the carrageenan-induced rat paw edema model, with efficacy rates of 79.41% and 82.39% at the 2nd and 4th hours, respectively, at a dose of 500 mg/kg (effects superior to those produced by indomethacin) (Lughmani et al., 2017). Daniel et al. (2009) reported that eugenol reduced paw edema and pleural exudates in the carrageenan-induced inflammation model.

**Molecular docking studies**: Computational analyses have revealed that eugenol fits into the active site of COX-2 via hydrogen bonding with Ser530 and hydrophobic interactions with multiple amino acid residues. Importantly, eugenol also demonstrates dual COX-2/5-LOX inhibitory activity, which may offer therapeutic advantages over selective COX-2 inhibitors by avoiding the shunting of arachidonic acid toward the leukotriene pathway (da Silva et al., 2020).

**Anti-inflammatory on RAW264.7 cells**: Ethanol extract and eugenol of S. aromaticum inhibited IL-1β, TNF-α, NO, and IL-6 secretion in LPS-stimulated RAW264.7 cells (Dibazar et al., 2015). Eugenol also inhibited COX-2 expression in this model (Kim et al., 2003). Porto et al. (2014) demonstrated that eugenol modulates pro-inflammatory mediator genes in murine peritoneal macrophages.

### 5.2 Antispasmodic and Smooth Muscle Relaxant Activity

The spasmolytic effect of eugenol on uterine smooth muscle is of direct relevance to its potential antidysmenorrhea application.

**Uterine smooth muscle relaxation**: Ponce-Monter et al. (2017) demonstrated that eugenol produces a concentration-dependent spasmolytic effect on isolated rat uterine strips. The relaxation was not reversed by blockers of β-adrenergic receptors, nitric oxide synthase, histamine receptors, or ATP-sensitive potassium channels, suggesting a mechanism independent of these pathways. The study concluded that eugenol produces spasmolytic effects through L-type calcium channel blockade.

**Calcium channel blockade**: The spasmolytic effect of eugenol on smooth muscle has been attributed to blockade of voltage-dependent calcium channels (VDCCs). This mechanism prevents calcium influx into smooth muscle cells, thereby inhibiting excitation-contraction coupling (Ponce-Monter et al., 2017; Leal-Cardoso et al., 2007). Seo et al. (2013) demonstrated that eugenol inhibits T-type Ca2+ channel isoforms (Cav3.1, Cav3.2, Cav3.3) with IC50 values of 463, 486, and 708 μM, respectively. Lee et al. (2008) showed that eugenol modulates Cav2.3 calcium channel currents.

**Vasorelaxant effects**: Eugenol induces concentration-dependent vasorelaxation in uterine arteries from both pregnant and non-pregnant animals, an effect partially mediated through activation of TRPV1 channels. Notably, the vasorelaxant effect of eugenol is amplified approximately three-fold in pregnancy, suggesting potential modulatory effects on uterine vascular tone (Biorxiv, 2020). Siqueira et al. (2022) characterized the vasodilator effect of eugenol in isolated human umbilical cord arteries, demonstrating involvement of VOCCs and K+ channels.

**Inhibition of arachidonic acid metabolism**: Eugenol has been reported to inhibit prostaglandin biosynthesis and thromboxane B2 formation induced by arachidonic acid in vitro (Batiha et al., 2020). It also inhibits 5-lipoxygenase (5-LOX) activity and leukotriene C4 production in human polymorphonuclear leukocytes (Raghavenra et al., 2006), providing a dual anti-inflammatory mechanism.

**Antispasmodic on bladder muscle**: Lee et al. (2024) demonstrated that eugenol exerts an antispasmodic effect on detrusor smooth muscle via blockade of VOCCs and/or M3R, providing a model for understanding eugenol's effects on smooth muscle contractility.

### 5.3 Analgesic Activity

Clove extracts and eugenol have demonstrated significant analgesic activity in experimental models.

**Acetic acid-induced writhing**: Taher et al. (2015) showed that aqueous and ethanolic extracts of clove exhibited dose-dependent analgesic effects in the hot plate test. The study by Hosseini et al. (2011) demonstrated that clove essential oil significantly inhibited acetic acid-induced abdominal constrictions in mice by 87.7%, which was superior to the effect produced by aspirin (77.7%).

**Formalin test**: Eugenol at doses of 50, 75, and 100 mg/kg exhibited significant antinociceptive activity in the formalin-induced paw-licking pain model, with more pronounced effects in the inflammatory phase (70.33%) than the neurogenic phase (42.22%) (Daniel et al., 2009).

**Thermal pain models**: In the hot plate test, eugenol showed a mild reduction in pain response latency at 100 mg/kg (Daniel et al., 2009). Park et al. (2006) elucidated the molecular mechanism for the local anesthetic action of eugenol in the rat trigeminal system, showing it blocks voltage-gated sodium channels similar to lidocaine.

**Analgesic strength**: Kurian et al. (2006) reported that eugenol at 100 mg/kg produced 92.73% inhibition of acetic acid-induced abdominal constrictions. Puspitasari et al. (2021) confirmed the analgesic effectiveness of clove flower extract on formalin-induced licking time in male mice.

### 5.4 Antioxidant Activity

Clove exhibits potent antioxidant activity, which may contribute to its therapeutic effects.

**Radical scavenging**: The antioxidant capacity of clove and eugenol has been demonstrated against DPPH, ABTS, and ferric chloride radicals, with IC50 values of 0.3257 mg/mL and 0.1967 mg/mL for clove and eugenol, respectively (de Oliveira et al., 2018). Selles et al. (2020) reported IC50 values of 4.82 × 10⁻² μg/mL for clove essential oil in the DPPH assay.

**Mechanism**: Eugenol scavenges free radicals by donating hydrogen atoms from its phenolic hydroxyl group, forming stable phenoxyl radicals through resonance. Gülçin (2011) demonstrated the in vitro antioxidant effectiveness of eugenol and discussed the structure-activity relationship, showing that eugenol allows donation of hydrogen atoms and subsequently fixes the phenoxyl radical.

**In vivo antioxidant**: Eugenol pretreatment (10.7 mg/kg/day for 15 days) in rats resulted in decreased lipid peroxidation indices, protein oxidation, and inflammatory markers, while improving antioxidant status by maintaining glutathione peroxidase (GPx), superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), and glutathione-S-transferase (GST) (Yogalakshmi et al., 2010). Kaur & Kaushal (2019) confirmed that eugenol inhibited expression of iNOS, COX-2, IL-6, TNF-α, and PGE2 while preventing antioxidant enzyme depletion.

### 5.5 Antiplatelet and Antithrombotic Activity

Srivastava & Malhotra (1991) demonstrated that acetyl eugenol, a component of clove oil, inhibits platelet aggregation and alters arachidonic acid metabolism in human blood platelets. Clove oil prevented platelet aggregation caused by platelet-activating factor, arachidonic acid, and collagen (Batiha et al., 2020). Eugenol was reported to prevent thromboxane B2 formation and platelet aggregation caused by arachidonic acid in vitro.

## 6. Acute Oral Toxicity of Clove

### 6.1 Toxicological Profile

Assessment of the safety profile of clove is essential for its development as a therapeutic agent. Multiple studies have investigated the acute oral toxicity of clove extracts and its major constituent eugenol.

**Agbaje et al. (2010)** determined the median lethal dose (LD50) of aqueous extract of S. aromaticum in mice. The LD50 was calculated as 263 mg/kg by the intraperitoneal route and 2,500 mg/kg by the oral route. These results indicate that the oral route is associated with significantly lower acute toxicity compared to parenteral administration. Histopathological studies showed effects on liver enzymes.

**EMA Assessment Report (2011)**: The European Medicines Agency compiled toxicity data from multiple studies:
- Essential oil (rat): oral LD50 range of 1.8–3.72 g/kg
- Eugenol (rat): oral LD50 of 2.68 g/kg; intraperitoneal LD50 of 800 mg/kg
- Eugenol (mouse): oral LD50 of 3 g/kg; intraperitoneal LD50 of 500 mg/kg
- A single oral dose of 5,000 mg/kg of clove essential oil in rats resulted in mortality within 24 hours, with postmortem findings including gastric and intestinal bleeding and pleural effusion.

**Johannah et al. (2016)** investigated the safety of Clovinol, a standardized polyphenolic extract of clove buds. Acute oral administration at 5 g/kg body weight in Wistar rats for 14 days produced no mortality or adverse effects. Subchronic administration (0.25, 0.5, and 1 g/kg/day for 90 days) established the No Observed Adverse Effect Level (NOAEL) as 1,000 mg/kg/day, the highest dose tested. No treatment-related changes were observed in body weight, organ weight, hematological parameters, clinical biochemistry, or histopathology.

**Tajuddin et al. (2004)** reported that oral administration of 500 mg/kg of 50% ethanolic extract of clove to mice produced no signs of mortality or gross behavioral changes.

**Santin et al. (2011)** demonstrated that both clove essential oil and eugenol exhibited no signs of acute toxicity in rats at doses that produced significant gastroprotective activity.

**Mishra & Singh (2008)** conducted a safety assessment of S. aromaticum flower bud extract with respect to testicular function in mice, finding no adverse effects at tested doses.

### 6.3 Regulatory Safety Classification

The FDA classifies clove buds, clove oil, eugenol, and oleoresins as GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe) for use as food additives (FDA, 2024). The WHO acceptable daily intake for clove oil is 2.5 mg/kg body weight (WHO/EMA, 2011). The US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has classified clove oil and eugenol as minimum risk pesticides (Batiha et al., 2020). The US FDA has approved clove buds, clove oil, and oleoresins as GRAS for use as food additives (Johannah et al., 2016).

### 6.4 OECD Guidelines for Acute Oral Toxicity Testing

The present study will follow the OECD Test Guideline 423 (Acute Toxic Class Method) for evaluation of acute oral toxicity (OECD, 2002). This method employs a stepwise procedure using three animals of a single sex (female) per step, with fixed doses of 5, 50, 300, and 2,000 mg/kg. The method is reproducible, uses relatively few animals, and enables classification of substances according to the Globally Harmonized System (GHS) for chemical classification and labeling. The up-and-down procedure (OECD Guideline 425) is an alternative that allows estimation of LD50 with confidence intervals (OECD, 2006).

## 7. Rationale and Justification

The high prevalence of dysmenorrhea, the significant proportion of women who do not respond adequately to or cannot tolerate current pharmacotherapy, and the well-documented traditional use of clove for menstrual disorders all support the investigation of clove as a potential antidysmenorrhea agent. The scientific literature provides robust evidence that:

1. Clove contains bioactive compounds, particularly eugenol, with established anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antispasmodic activities
2. Eugenol inhibits prostaglandin synthesis via COX-2 suppression and 5-LOX inhibition
3. Eugenol relaxes smooth muscle through calcium channel blockade
4. Clove extracts exhibit a favorable safety profile with high oral LD50 values
5. The oral route of administration is feasible and associated with low acute toxicity at therapeutic doses
6. Systematic reviews support the efficacy of medicinal plants for dysmenorrhea with favorable side effect profiles compared to NSAIDs

Despite this scientific basis, there is a gap in the literature regarding systematic evaluation of standardized clove extracts specifically for antidysmenorrhea effects using appropriate animal models, particularly the oxytocin-induced writhing model. The present study aims to address this gap.

## 8. Study Objectives

### 8.1 General Objective

To evaluate the acute oral toxicity and antidysmenorrhea effects of hydroalcoholic extract of Syzygium aromaticum (clove) in experimental animal models.

### 8.2 Specific Objectives

1. To prepare and characterize a hydroalcoholic extract of Syzygium aromaticum flower buds
2. To determine the acute oral toxicity profile and median lethal dose (LD50) of the extract in accordance with OECD Guideline 423
3. To evaluate the effect of the extract on uterine contractility using an isolated uterine tissue model
4. To assess the antidysmenorrhea effect using an in vivo dysmenorrhea model (oxytocin-induced abdominal writhing in mice)
5. To compare the efficacy of the extract with standard NSAID therapy (ibuprofen)

### 8.3 Study Hypotheses

**Null hypothesis (H0)**: The hydroalcoholic extract of Syzygium aromaticum does not possess significant acute oral toxicity nor antidysmenorrhea effects in experimental animal models.

**Alternative hypothesis (H1)**: The hydroalcoholic extract of Syzygium aromaticum possesses significant antidysmenorrhea effects with an acceptable acute oral toxicity profile.
