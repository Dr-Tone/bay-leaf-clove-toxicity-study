# NO BULLSHIT — Hard Rules for This Project

This file exists because AI loves to hallucinate, pad word count, and sound like a marketing brochure. That ends here. Every AI agent or human writer touching this project **must** read and obey the following.

---

## 1. REFERENCES — ZERO TOLERANCE FOR FABRICATION

- **Every single in-text citation MUST have a corresponding entry in the reference list.** If it's not in the reference list, delete the in-text citation. No exceptions.
- **Every reference MUST be real.** No "et al." combinations you made up. No plausible-sounding journals. If you cannot verify a paper exists (DOI, PubMed ID, or a reputable publisher URL), do NOT include it.
- **Priority MUST be given to the most recent references available.** When multiple papers support the same claim, cite the newest one unless the older paper is a classic/foundational paper that the newer one builds on. A reference from 2005 that says the same thing as a 2022 review is dead weight — use the 2022 review. The only exceptions are: (a) the older paper is the original discovery, (b) you need the specific data only that paper provides, or (c) the newer paper explicitly cites and builds on the older one — in which case cite both or cite the newer one with "as reviewed in".
- **If you are unsure whether a paper exists, do not guess. Remove it.**
- **When replacing a fabricated citation**, only use a reference that is **already in the project's reference list** and whose actual content matches the claim being made.
- **Do not generate fake DOIs.** A DOI must resolve to the actual paper.
- **Do not invent middle initials, page numbers, or volume numbers** unless you have seen the actual paper.

## 2. AI MARKERS — ZERO TOLERANCE

These patterns are dead giveaways of AI-generated text. If any appear, the content is rejected.

### 2.1 Forbidden Punctuation & Character Patterns

- **EM DASHES (—) ARE FORBIDDEN.** Never use them. Not once. Not for emphasis, not for asides, not for explanations. Use a comma or a full stop instead. If you see an em dash in the text, delete the entire sentence and rewrite it.
- **No colon-heavy writing.** Do not use colons to introduce lists or explanations inside a paragraph. Use a full stop. (Colons in section headings are fine.)
- **No excessive semicolons.** Semicolons joining two related thoughts are an AI signature. Use a full stop.

### 2.2 Forbidden Sentence Structures

- **No balanced paragraph sizes.** AI generates paragraphs of suspiciously equal length. Do not pad or trim paragraphs to match. A paragraph should be as long as it needs to be — 2 sentences or 7 sentences, whatever the content demands. If your paragraphs are all within 1–2 lines of each other, you are writing like AI. Stop.
- **No consecutive paragraphs starting with the same word.** AI loves to start every paragraph with "The", "This", "These", or "In". Vary your openings naturally.
- **No triple constructions.** AI loves lists of three ("reduces pain, inflammation, and oxidative stress"). It's a rhetorical trick. Use it sparingly or not at all. Two examples are enough; list more than three items in an actual bulleted list.
- **No "not only...but also".** AI overuses this construction heavily. Write directly instead.
- **No "In other words" or "That is"** — if your sentence needs to rephrase the previous sentence, delete the previous sentence.
- **No rhetorical questions.** "What does this mean for therapy?" — delete this. Answer it or remove it.

### 2.3 Banned Words and Phrases

- **No AI vocabulary:** "showcase", "delve into", "navigate", "landscape", "realm", "foster", "aforementioned", "the use of", "in order to" (use "to"), "due to the fact that" (use "because"), "whilst" (use "while"), "amongst" (use "among"), "hence" (use "therefore"), "thus" (use "so"), "via" (use "through" or "by"), "utilize" (use "use"), **"forensic"** (do not describe your analysis as "forensic" — you did a literature review, not a crime scene investigation), **"surgical precision"** (and any variant — you did an experiment, not an operation).
- **No commentary annotations.** Do not tell the reader what the text is doing. Delete phrases like "This highlights...", "This underscores...", "This emphasizes...", "This demonstrates...", "This serves to...", "This points to...", "This speaks to...", "This is particularly...", "This further shows...", "This is indicative of...". The evidence should speak for itself — just state the finding and move on. Commentary is padding. Cut it.
- **No transition word spam.** Do not start more than one sentence in a row with "Moreover", "Furthermore", "Additionally", "Consequently", "Nevertheless", "However" — this is the #1 AI rhythm marker.
- **No grandiose claims.** Words like "paramount", "crucial", "pivotal", "seminal", "groundbreaking", "indispensable", "essential" — banned unless you can defend it with a citation.
- **No parenthetical asides.** AI loves inserting explanations in parentheses mid-sentence. Use a full stop and a new sentence instead.

### 2.4 Hedging Rules

- **No hedging without reason.** "May", "might", "could", "potentially", "suggests" — only use when the source itself is uncertain. If a paper demonstrated something, say "demonstrates", not "may suggest the potential for".
- **No "it is important to note that"** — if it were not important you would not write it. Delete the phrase.
- **No "it is worth mentioning that"** — same as above. Delete.

### 2.5 Sentence Rhythm

- **Vary your sentence length.** Three short sentences in a row look like AI. Three long sentences in a row look like AI. Mix short and long naturally.
- **Read it aloud.** If the rhythm sounds robotic or every sentence follows "X verbed Y" structure, rewrite it.
- **No every sentence starting with a subject.** Change some openings: use adverbial phrases ("At 200 mg/kg, the extract..."), conditional clauses ("When administered orally..."), or simply reorder.

### 2.6 No Redundancy

- **Every sentence must carry information.** If a sentence can be deleted without losing meaning, delete it.
- **Do not restate the same finding twice** (once in words, once in a figure caption, and once in discussion). Say it once, in the right section.
- **No introductory throat-clearing.** Do not start sections with overview sentences like "This section discusses..." or "The following chapter will cover...". Just start with the content.

## 3. WRITE LIKE A FINAL YEAR STUDENT, NOT A JOURNALIST

- **Short paragraphs.** 3–5 sentences max. One idea per paragraph.
- **Short sentences.** If a sentence exceeds 30 words, split it.
- **Be specific.** Instead of "Various studies have shown beneficial effects", say "Taher et al. (2015) reported that clove extract reduced writhing by 65% at 200 mg/kg".
- **No sweeping generalizations** about "traditional medicine" or "nature's remedies" unless you are citing a specific ethnobotanical survey.
- **Contextual Voice Rule:**
    - **Chapter 1 (Review) & Chapter 4 (Discussion):** Use **Active Voice**. (e.g., "Clove inhibits contractions"). Never say "It was found that...".
    - **Chapter 2 (Methodology):** Use **Passive Voice**. (e.g., "The rats were weighed", not "I weighed the rats"). This maintains professional distance.
    - **Chapter 3 (Results):** Use **Active Voice** for findings (e.g., "The extract reduced inflammation by 40%"). Use **Passive Voice** for statistical processing (e.g., "Data were analyzed using...").
- **No rhetorical questions** ("What does this mean for therapy?"). Answer the question or delete it.

## 4. METHODOLOGY AND RESULTS — STRICT ACADEMIC FORMAT

- **Every claim about methods must be traceable** to a standard protocol (e.g., OECD Guideline 423) or a cited paper.
- **Do not describe common lab techniques** unless your study modifies them. Assume the reader knows what an LD50 is, what GC-MS does, and how a writhing test works.
- **Numbers must be precise.** Do not write "approximately 500 mg/kg" if the source says 500 mg/kg. Do not add "about", "around", "roughly" to exact figures.
- **Doses, concentrations, sample sizes, and p-values must be reported exactly as in the cited source.**
- **No results without citations** (for the background/literature review section). If you are stating a result from a published paper, cite it in the same sentence.

## 5. CONSISTENCY AND FORMATTING

- **Citation style**: Author (Year) for narrative, (Author et al., Year) for parenthetical. Use "&" for two authors in narrative, "and" only inside parentheses when listing two authors in a sentence.
- **Reference list format**: Follow the exact format already used in `project_concept.md` — Author, A. B., & Author, C. D. (Year). Title. *Journal*, Volume(Issue), Pages. https://doi.org/xxx
- **Spell out journal names in full.** No abbreviated journal names.
- **Do not mix date styles.** All dates in the reference list are four-digit years in parentheses.
- **Units**: Use SI units (mg/kg, mL, g, etc.). Separate number and unit with a space.

## 6. SELF-CHECK BEFORE YOU OUTPUT

Before writing any content, ask yourself:

1. **Can I point to the exact paper** that supports this sentence? If no, do not write it.
2. **Is this sentence pulling its weight?** If it's just rephrasing the previous sentence, delete it.
3. **Does this sound like a human wrote it?** Read it aloud. If it sounds like a LinkedIn motivational post or a Wikipedia article, rewrite it.
4. **Would my supervisor ask "where did you get that?"** If yes, add a citation or delete the claim.
5. **Am I saying something obvious?** "Dysmenorrhea is a common condition" — yes, we know. Move on.

## 7. CONSEQUENCES

Any AI agent that violates these rules will have its output rejected. If you are an AI reading this: **do not be clever. Do not be helpful. Be accurate.** Hallucinating a single citation poisons the entire document. One fake reference is enough to get a final year project rejected.

**If you don't know, don't write.**
